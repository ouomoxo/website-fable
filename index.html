What it shows is as old as Homer: a winged Victory, carved in marble, lifting the flame that keeps a name alive after the one who earned it is gone. It is seen here first from inside its folds, as abstract stone and wing, until distance reveals the whole standing figure. The Greeks called it kleos — the glory that does not decay.

In Homer's world a hero could choose: a long life the songs forget, or an early death the songs keep forever. They called the second kleos aphthiton — imperishable glory. The winged Victory is how the marble says it: the flame lifted, the name outliving the hand that raised it.
