# Research — the historical monument behind AFTER EMELYN

This project is an original digital monument *inspired by* a real work of
funerary sculpture. This file records the facts the project rests on, how
confident we are in each, and where the poetic layer deliberately departs
from the record. Facts and poetry are kept separate; nothing in the site's
epilogue states anything not supported below.

## The work

**Angel of Grief** — full title as the sculptor gave it:
*The Angel of Grief Weeping Over the Dismantled Altar of Life.*

A winged figure collapsed forward over an altar, face buried and hidden,
one bare arm flung across the stone, wings fallen — grief rendered as
total structural collapse rather than dignified mourning.

## The people

- **William Wetmore Story** (1819–1895). American sculptor, poet and
  essayist. Son of U.S. Supreme Court Justice Joseph Story. Trained as a
  lawyer in Boston; abandoned law for sculpture and lived most of his
  adult life as an expatriate in Rome, where his apartment in Palazzo
  Barberini was a centre of the Anglo-American artistic community. Henry
  James later wrote his biography (*William Wetmore Story and His
  Friends*, 1903). — **Confidence: high** (standard art-historical record).
- **Emelyn Story**, née Eldredge, his wife, who managed much of the
  couple's celebrated social and intellectual life in Rome. She died in
  Rome in **1894**. — **Confidence: high** for the year; exact date not
  asserted anywhere in this project.

## The event the site narrates

- After Emelyn's death Story, in deep grief and by then in his
  mid-seventies, carved the Angel of Grief as her memorial. It is
  regarded as **his last major work**. — **Confidence: high.**
- The monument stands over the Story grave in the **Non-Catholic
  Cemetery in Rome** (Cimitero Acattolico, historically the "Protestant
  Cemetery," near the Pyramid of Cestius). — **Confidence: high.**
- Story died the following year, **1895**, and is **buried beside her,
  beneath the same monument**. — **Confidence: high.**
- The couple's early tie to that cemetery came through the death of a
  young son buried there decades earlier. — **Confidence: moderate**;
  not used in site copy.
- The statue has been replicated many times in cemeteries worldwide
  (the Stanford University mausoleum replica is the best known).
  — **Confidence: high**; not used in site copy.

## What the project does NOT claim

- This site's monument is **not a scan and not a reconstruction** of the
  original sculpture. It is an original, procedurally carved monument
  that borrows the *posture of grief* — the collapsed figure, the fallen
  wing, the hidden face, the bare hanging hand — and the emotional logic
  of the work, not its literal geometry.
- No inscription text from the real grave is reproduced. The pedestal
  faces in this project are deliberately blank.
- Exact day-level dates, studio locations, and quotations are not
  asserted anywhere in the experience.
